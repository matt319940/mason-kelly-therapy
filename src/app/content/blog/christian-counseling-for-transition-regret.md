Regret is one of the heaviest things a person can carry — especially regret tangled up with identity, faith, and the body. If you're navigating transition regret and you want a space that honors your faith, please know: you are not alone, and you are not beyond grace.

## Grief is allowed here

Transition regret often carries real grief — for time, for relationships, for a future you once pictured. In faith communities, grief can sometimes get rushed, as though hope requires us to skip past sorrow. It doesn't.

In counseling, we make room for grief *and* hope to exist together. We don't force either one. Sometimes the most healing thing is simply to let the grief be witnessed without judgment.

## Shame is not the same as repentance

One of the most freeing distinctions in faith-centered work is the difference between **shame** and healthy reflection. Shame says, *you are bad.* It isolates and condemns. That's not the voice of grace.

Healthy reflection — and, for those who frame it this way, repentance — is oriented toward freedom and restoration, not self-punishment. Together we can explore forgiveness in a way that releases rather than crushes.

## Identity beyond a single chapter

You are more than any one chapter of your story. Part of this work is rediscovering who you are now — your values, your relationships, your sense of self — with self-compassion instead of self-condemnation.

For clients who want it, Scripture and prayer can be a source of steadiness in that process. They're offered at your invitation, never imposed, and woven together with evidence-based clinical care.

## You set the pace

This is voluntary, client-led work. Faith is a resource I can offer, never a result I require. You decide what to explore and when.
