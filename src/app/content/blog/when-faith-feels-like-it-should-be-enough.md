If you're a person of faith who struggles with depression or anxiety, you may carry an extra weight that others don't talk about: **guilt for struggling at all.** A quiet voice that says, *"If my faith were stronger, I wouldn't feel this way."*

I want to gently push back on that voice.

## Struggling is not a failure of faith

Depression and anxiety are not measures of your spiritual worth. They are real, often biological, and deeply human experiences. Some of the most faithful people in Scripture wrestled with despair, fear, and exhaustion. Struggle and faith have always lived side by side.

Believing you *shouldn't* feel anxious or low rarely makes those feelings go away. More often, it just adds shame on top of pain — and shame tends to make everything heavier.

## Faith and good care are not rivals

Here is a more freeing way to see it: God works through good clinical care, too. Choosing therapy isn't a sign of weak faith any more than seeing a doctor for a broken arm would be.

In faith-integrated therapy, we can hold both at once:

- **Evidence-based tools** — like cognitive-behavioral approaches and clinical hypnotherapy — to quiet anxious thinking and lift the weight of depression.
- **Faith as an anchor of hope**, woven in at your pace, rather than a source of guilt.

## Small steps count

Healing rarely happens all at once. It tends to come through small, steady steps — naming what you feel, learning new coping skills, and being met with compassion instead of judgment. Many people begin to feel some relief within the first several sessions.
