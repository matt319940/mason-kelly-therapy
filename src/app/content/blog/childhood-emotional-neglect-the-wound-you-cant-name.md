Some wounds come from what happened to us. Others come from what *didn't* happen — the comfort that never came, the feelings no one asked about, the sense that our inner world simply didn't register. This is **childhood emotional neglect** (CEN), and because it's defined by absence, it's remarkably easy to overlook.

## Why it's so hard to name

People who grew up with emotional neglect often say things like, *"I had a fine childhood — nothing bad happened."* And often, on the surface, that's true. There may have been no abuse, no obvious trauma.

But when a child's emotions are consistently ignored, dismissed, or left unattended, they learn a quiet lesson: *my feelings don't matter.* That lesson can shape an entire adult life.

## Common signs in adulthood

- A persistent sense of emptiness or feeling "different," without knowing why
- Difficulty identifying or trusting your own emotions
- Being hard on yourself; a harsh inner critic
- Discomfort relying on others, or guilt when you have needs
- A tendency toward people-pleasing or over-responsibility (sometimes called [codependency](/services/codependency-therapy))

If several of these feel familiar, you're not broken — and you're not alone.

## Healing begins with permission

The good news is that what was missed can, in many ways, be learned later. Healing from CEN often involves:

- Learning to notice and name your emotions without judgment
- Building self-compassion to soften the inner critic
- Practicing healthy boundaries and allowing yourself to have needs
- Grieving what wasn't there, so it stops quietly running the show

For those who want it, faith can be a steadying part of that work — grounding your worth in being loved as you are, not in what you do for others.

This is gentle, paced work, and it's very possible. If it resonates, you can explore [trauma-informed therapy](/services/trauma-ptsd-therapy) or simply [reach out](/contact) to talk.
