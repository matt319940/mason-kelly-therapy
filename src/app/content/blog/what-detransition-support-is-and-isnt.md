If you've searched for help with detransition or transition regret, you may have run into a confusing mix of strong opinions. It can be hard to find a simple, honest answer to a basic question: *what does this kind of therapy actually involve?*

Let me try to answer that plainly.

## Support that follows your goals

Detransition and transition-regret support is **voluntary, client-led talk therapy**. That means a few specific things:

- **You set the goals.** I don't arrive with an agenda for your life or your body. My role is to understand what *you* want and to walk alongside you toward it.
- **You set the pace.** You can explore something, pause, change direction, or stop entirely. Nothing happens without your readiness.
- **It's trauma-informed.** We prioritize safety, trust, and choice — the foundations of any ethical therapeutic relationship.

This is the same client-centered approach that defines good therapy for grief, trauma, or any other deeply personal struggle.

## What it is *not*

It is **not** "conversion therapy." It does not try to change anyone's identity, and it never pressures you toward a predetermined outcome. There is no "fixing," no "curing," and no script you're expected to follow.

That distinction matters ethically and legally — but more than that, it matters *for you*. Coercion has no place in healing. A space that pushes you toward someone else's conclusion isn't a safe one.

## Why people seek this kind of support

People come to detransition support for many reasons: grief over time or relationships, questions about identity, the physical and emotional weight of medical decisions, or a desire to reconnect with their faith. Some want to talk through regret. Some are simply looking for a therapist who won't judge them for asking hard questions.

Whatever brings you in, you deserve a licensed clinician who will listen without an agenda.
